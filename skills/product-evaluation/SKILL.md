This skill applies five product design frameworks to evaluate and shape product decisions. Use it when assessing features, prioritizing roadmap items, reviewing positioning, or making strategic product calls.

The user provides a product decision, feature proposal, or strategic question. Apply all five lenses and surface the kill test.

## 1. THE IVE LENS: Design as Inevitability

### Core Principles

**Simplicity is not minimalism — it is inevitability.**

> "True simplicity is, well, you just keep on going and going until you get to the point where you go, 'Yeah, well, of course.' Where there's no rational alternative."

Simplicity is semantic — the design communicates what it IS. Not fewer things. The only possible solution.

**Test: Does this design feel like the only way it could be? Or does it feel like one option among many?**

**Care is detectable.**

> "People can sense care and can sense carelessness. This relates to respect for each other and carelessness is personally offensive."

People respond to "something much bigger than the object" — they sense the team behind products who care about unseen details.

**Test: Would we be proud of this if someone saw the implementation? The animation timing? The error state?**

**Materials dictate form.**

> "It is only when you personally work with a material with your hands, that you come to understand its true nature, its characteristics, its attributes, and — very importantly — its potential."

**The "material" is your product's core medium. What forms emerge naturally from it? Don't impose patterns from other product categories onto yours.**

**Design and engineering are inseparable.**

> "We develop forms that achieve an integrity between external surfaces and internal components."

### Ive's Evaluation Checklist

1. Does it feel inevitable, or like one option among many?
2. Does it communicate its purpose through its form?
3. Did the form emerge from the material, or was it imposed?
4. Would we be proud of every detail, including the ones users won't see?
5. Have we iterated enough, or did we ship the first version that worked?

## 2. THE JOBS LENS: Strategic Focus

### Focus = Saying No to Good Ideas

> "People think focus means saying yes to the thing you've got to focus on. But that's not what it means at all. It means saying no to the hundred other good ideas."

**Test: If we could only ship THREE features, which three? Everything else is a "good idea" we're saying no to.**

### Technology Married with Liberal Arts

The soul of a product — its personality, voice, and emotional intelligence — isn't "nice to have." In many cases, it IS the product. The craft and care in how the product communicates is the calligraphy.

### Launch as Narrative, Not Feature Demo

Don't present features. Present a worldview shift, then show how the product is the inevitable expression of that shift.

**Don't say "here are 15 features." Say "here's what's broken in the world, and here's the only way to fix it."**

## 3. THE VOHRA ENGINE: Finding Product-Market Fit

### The Sean Ellis / Superhuman Survey

**Core question:** "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?"

- Very disappointed / Somewhat disappointed / Not disappointed
- **Benchmark: 40% "very disappointed" = PMF**

Additional questions:

- Q2: "What type of people would most benefit?" → Find your HXC (high-expectation customer)
- Q3: "What is the main benefit you receive?" → Loyalty driver
- Q4: "How can we improve?" → Surface blockers

### Administration Rules

- Send ~21 days after signup
- User must have used product at least twice in preceding two weeks
- Directionally accurate at ~40 respondents
- Never re-survey the same user

### The Four-Step Engine

1. **Segment to find your people.** Calculate PMF score per persona. Segmentation alone moved Superhuman from 22% to 32%.
2. **The strategic filter.** IGNORE "not disappointed" users. FOCUS ON "somewhat disappointed" users whose Q3 benefit ALIGNS with your core value. Examine their Q4 improvement suggestions.
3. **50/50 roadmap split.** 50% double down on what lovers love. 50% fix blockers for fence-sitters. Neither alone works.
4. **Repeat continuously.** Track PMF score as primary OKR.

## 4. THE CAGAN FRAMEWORK: Product Discovery

### The Four Big Risks (address BEFORE building)

| Risk                   | Question                                | Owner           |
| ---------------------- | --------------------------------------- | --------------- |
| **Value**              | Will customers buy/use it?              | Product Manager |
| **Usability**          | Can users figure it out?                | Designer        |
| **Feasibility**        | Can we build it?                        | Engineer        |
| **Business Viability** | Does it work for sales, legal, finance? | Product Manager |

### Discovery Techniques

- **Customer Discovery:** 8-20 "earlyvangelists" for B2C. 6 reference customers from a single segment validates PMF.
- **Prototyping:** User prototypes (low-fi → high-fi), feasibility prototypes, live data prototypes, hybrid (Zappos model).
- **Value Testing:** Fake door test, landing page test, payment willingness, behavior change measurement.

### Key Principle

> "Prototype as if you know you're right, but test as if you know you're wrong."

## 5. THE DUNFORD FRAMEWORK: Positioning

### The 5+1 Components (mandatory sequence)

1. **Competitive Alternatives** — "What would customers do if we didn't exist?"
2. **Unique Attributes** — Features/capabilities alternatives lack. Require proof.
3. **Value & Proof** — Features → benefits → outcomes. Chain each unique attribute through to a measurable outcome.
4. **Target Customer** — Traits of highest-affinity customers. Buy quickly, rarely negotiate, refer others.
5. **Market Category** — Frame that triggers assumptions where your strengths are obvious.
6. **+1 Trends** — Layer on only if authentic.

### Three Positioning Strategies

- **Head to Head:** Requires capital. Not for startups.
- **Big Fish, Small Pond:** Dominate a subsegment. Best for most startups.
- **Create a New Game:** Most difficult. 90% of recently public tech companies positioned in existing markets.

## SYNTHESIS: The Evaluation Protocol

When evaluating any product decision, run it through all five lenses:

### The Five Questions

1. **IVE:** Does this feel inevitable? Is there no rational alternative?
2. **JOBS:** If we could only ship three things, would this be one?
3. **VOHRA:** Will this move our "very disappointed" score? Does it double down on what lovers love, or fix a blocker for fence-sitters?
4. **CAGAN:** Have we validated value, usability, feasibility, and viability BEFORE building?
5. **DUNFORD:** Does this strengthen our positioning or dilute it?

### The Kill Test

If a feature fails ANY of these, it needs justification:

- **Ive:** Doesn't feel inevitable → Why are we building it?
- **Jobs:** Doesn't pass the "top 3" test → Cut it.
- **Vohra:** Won't move PMF score → Deprioritize.
- **Cagan:** Haven't validated demand → Test before building.
- **Dunford:** Dilutes positioning → Dangerous.
